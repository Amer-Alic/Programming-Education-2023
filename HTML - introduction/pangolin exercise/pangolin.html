Pangolin Intro Pangolins, sometimes known as scaly anteaters, are mammals of the order Pholidota (from the ancient Greek φολῐ́ς, "horned shell"). One extant family, Manidae, has three genera: Manis, Phataginus, and Smutsia. Manis consists of four species which are found in Asia, while Phataginus and Smutsia include two species each, which are all found in sub-Saharan Africa. These species range in size from 30 to 100 cm (12 to 39 in). Several extinct species of pangolin are also known. Description Pangolins have large, protective keratin scales that cover their skin; they are the only known mammals with this feature. They live in hollow trees or burrows, depending on the species. Pangolins are nocturnal, and their diet consists of mainly from ants and termites, which they catch with their long tongues. They tend to be solitary animals, they date only for mating and give a litter of one to three offspring, which they raise for about two years. Poaching Pangolins are threatened by poaching (for their meat and scales, which are used in traditional Chinese medicine) and large deforestation of their natural habitats, and are the most traded mammals in the world. From January 2020, from eight pangolin species, three (Manis culionensis, M. pentadactyla and M. javanica) are listed as critically endangered, three (Phataginus tricuspis, Manis crassicaudata and Smutsia gigantea) are listed as endangered, and two (Phataginus tetradactyla and Smutsia) are listed as v. List of endangered species of the International Union for Conservation of Nature.
